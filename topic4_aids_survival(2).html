1 Introduction

Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS) is a disease that damages the immune system and makes the body more vulnerable to infections and other illnesses. Although medical treatment has improved over the years, AIDS still causes many deaths around the world.

Studying survival after diagnosis helps researchers understand how long patients live after developing the disease and what factors influence survival. Some patients die during follow-up while others are still alive at the end of the study. When this happens, the outcome is called time-to-event data, and survival analysis is the appropriate statistical method.

This project investigates survival outcomes of AIDS patients in Australia. The analysis focuses on how factors such as age, sex, state of residence, and transmission category affect survival time.

The survival analysis methods used in this project are :

  • Kaplan–Meier survival estimation
  • Log-rank test
  • Cox proportional hazards model
  • Proportional hazards assumption test

These methods help describe survival patterns and identify factors associated with the risk of death.

1.1 Objective

The main objective of this study is to investigate the survival outcomes of AIDS patients in Australia.

The specific objectives are:

  • To explore the dataset using descriptive statistics and graphs.
  • To estimate survival probabilities using Kaplan–Meier curves.
  • To compare survival between groups using the log-rank test.
  • To identify factors affecting survival using the Cox proportional hazards model.
  • To check whether the proportional hazards assumption is satisfied.

4 Model Formulation

Survival analysis studies the time until an event occurs.

The survival function is defined as:

S(t) = P(T > t)

This represents the probability that a patient survives longer than time t.

The Cox proportional hazards model is written as:

h(t|X) = h₀(t) exp(β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + …)

where:

  • h(t|X) is the hazard of death
  • h₀(t) is the baseline hazard
  • X represents explanatory variables

The predictors used in this study are:

  • age
  • sex
  • state
  • transmission category

4.1 Kaplan–Meier Survival Curve

print(p1)

Interpretation

The Kaplan–Meier curve shows that the survival probability of AIDS patients decreases over time. At the start, all 2843 patients were alive, so the survival probability begins near 1. As time goes on, the curve keeps going down, which means more patients die during follow-up. By around 2 years, survival has dropped a lot, and after 4 to 6 years only a small number of patients are still alive. The number at risk also becomes smaller over time, from 2843 at the start to 459 at 2 years, 47 at 4 years, and 6 at 6 years. This shows that survival became lower as follow-up time increased.

The overall model is statistically significant (Likelihood ratio test p < 0.001), which means the variables in the model help explain survival, although the concordance of 0.567 suggests the model has only a modest ability to predict who survives longer

4.6 Proportional Hazards Assumption

ph_test <- cox.zph(cox_model)
ph_test
        chisq df       p
age     22.71  1 1.9e-06
sex      2.72  1  0.0991
state   17.03  3  0.0007
T.categ 34.27  7 1.5e-05
GLOBAL  59.69 12 2.6e-08

Interpretation

The proportional hazards test checks whether the effect of each variable stays the same over time in the Cox model. the global p-value is 2.6 × 10⁻⁸, which is less than 0.05, so the model does not fully satisfy this assumption. This means some variables change their effect over time. In particular, age (p = 1.9 × 10⁻⁶), state (p = 0.0007), and transmission category (p = 1.5 × 10⁻⁵) do not satisfy the assumption, so their effects on survival are not constant during follow-up. However, sex (p = 0.0991) is greater than 0.05, so sex does not show a serious problem and its effect can be treated as fairly constant over time. Overall, this means the Cox model is useful, but it should be interpreted carefully because some predictors do not meet the proportional hazards assumption.

Interpretation

The Schoenfeld residual test was used to check the proportional hazards assumption of the Cox model. The global test is significant (p = 2.57 × 10⁻⁸), which suggests that the proportional hazards assumption may not hold for the model. Age, state, and transmission category have p-values less than 0.05, indicating that their effects may change over time. However, sex has a p-value greater than 0.05, suggesting that the proportional hazards assumption is reasonable for this variable.

5 Recommendations

The government should strengthen early HIV testing and early treatment programs. The analysis showed that survival probability decreases over time after diagnosis. Detecting HIV earlier and starting treatment quickly can help patients live longer and improve their quality of life.

Health services should give special attention to older patients. The results showed that age increases the risk of death, meaning older patients have poorer survival outcomes. Regular monitoring and targeted healthcare support for older patients may help reduce this risk.

Prevention programs should focus on major transmission groups. The study showed that some transmission categories have different levels of risk. Public health programs should continue to promote awareness, safe behaviors, and prevention strategies for high-risk groups to reduce new infections.

The government should improve long-term care and follow-up for AIDS patients. Since survival decreases over time, continuous medical care, treatment support, and regular monitoring are important to improve patient outcomes.

The government should strengthen data collection and monitoring systems for HIV/AIDS patients. Better data can help health authorities understand survival patterns and design more effective policies and healthcare programs.

6 Conclusion

This study examined the survival outcomes of AIDS patients in Australia using the Aids2 dataset. The analysis showed that the survival probability of AIDS patients decreases over time after diagnosis. Many patients survived for a short period, while fewer patients survived for many years.

The results showed that age is an important factor affecting survival. Older patients have a higher risk of death compared with younger patients. The analysis also showed that some transmission categories have different levels of risk, meaning that survival outcomes are not the same for all groups.

The comparison between males and females showed that females appeared to have slightly better survival, but the difference was not statistically significant. This means sex did not have a strong effect on survival in this study.

The results show that survival of AIDS patients is strongly related to age and transmission category. Survival becomes lower as more time passes after diagnosis. These findings help us better understand how long patients live after diagnosis and can help health authorities plan better treatment and care programs for AIDS patients.
